3. Occassion prompt library

If you wanted “occasion suitability probabilities” the traditional way, you’d need a labeled dataset to train a classifier.
Instead, prompts let you create an occasion scoring function without training by using CLIP’s learned knowledge.

Because CLIP learned a shared embedding space where: images of “wedding guest dresses” and the text “wedding guest dress” end up close together.
CLIP was trained on huge datasets of image–text pairs so that the correct caption matches the image better than random captions.
==> zero-shot classification / prompt-based scoring

embedding("wedding guest dress") 
      ↕
embedding(image_of_formal_dress)

If the image looks like what CLIP has learned corresponds to wedding attire, the vectors will be close.
If the image is:
ripped jeans → far away
hoodie → far away
formal dress → close
elegant blazer → somewhat close

So the system doesn’t “decide” what clothing fits a wedding —
it measures how visually similar the item is to the learned concept of “wedding attire”.

CLIP does not “understand weddings” like a human.
It understands: visual patterns associated with text used on the internet
That’s enough for: ranking, recommendation, suitability estimation
And it’s perfectly acceptable for a thesis.

!!!! Occasion embeddings represent visual prototypes, and products are scored by how visually close they are to those prototypes.

Robust library:

- Criteria for defining the prompts
- X prompts per occasion
    3–5 prompts per occasion is a sweet spot that balances:
        robustness (less sensitivity to wording),
        coverage (different styles within the same occasion),
        and efficiency (no unnecessary noise or computation).
        many prompts = inneficient (ex: 10 occasions × 10 prompts = 100 prompt comparisons per item)
    Each occasion is represented by a compact prompt ensemble that captures stylistic variability while remaining computationally efficient.

        Beyond ~5 prompts: prompts become redundant, some introduce noise or unintended bias, improvements plateau, explanations get harder
        More prompts ≠ more information.
            Each occasion is represented by a small ensemble of three to five prompts. This choice reflects a trade-off between robustness and efficiency: multiple prompts reduce sensitivity to individual wording choices and capture stylistic variation within an occasion, while keeping the ensemble compact avoids redundant signals and unnecessary computational overhead.

- X Q of occasions 
    Fewer than 6 → too broad (everything becomes “formal vs casual”)
    More than ~10 → becomes hard to define cleanly, overlaps explode, evaluation gets messy
    (6-10)

PCA plot suggested that prompts were very far away from the image embeddings. 
This PCA plot is not strong evidence that the approach “isn’t working.” Two important things can be true at the same time:
- Your retrieval/scoring with CLIP can work perfectly well in the original 512-D space, and
- A 2-D PCA visualization can look “broken” because PCA often separates modalities (text vs image) along the first principal component.

A. PCA often separates modality (text vs image), not meaning
When you run PCA on mixed embeddings, the biggest variance direction is often:
“text vectors vs image vectors”
So PCA₁ becomes a “domain axis”. That pushes all text points to one side and images to the other, even if cosine similarity in the full space is still meaningful.


C. Missing normalization (very common) --> it is normalized!!! 
For cosine similarity / dot-product retrieval you should L2-normalize both:
image embeddings
text embeddings
If you normalize images but not text (or vice versa), projections and similarity behave weirdly, and PCA can exaggerate the gap.

D. PCA plot scale is arbitrary and misleading
Even if everything is correct, PCA axes can make it look like “they’re far apart,” but what matters is relative closeness.
A better sanity check is:
“Does the prompt ‘wedding guest outfit’ retrieve wedding-like dresses near the top?”
Not whether points overlap in a 2-D projection.
